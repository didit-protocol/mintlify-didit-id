---
title: "Wallets and presenting"
description: "Learn what a wallet is and how selective disclosure lets you share only needed facts."
---

A wallet is an app that holds your credentials. It is similar to Apple Wallet holding a boarding pass or payment card. Instead of storing only images, this wallet stores signed digital proofs.

The holder is the person or system that controls the wallet. Presenting means showing a credential to a verifier, which is the app or organization checking the proof. If a hotel asks for proof that you are over 18, your wallet can present that fact.

The wallet does not need to show your full birth date if the credential supports selective disclosure.

Selective disclosure means you choose exactly which facts to share. It is like covering parts of a paper ID with your finger before showing it. The hidden parts stay hidden, and the shown parts can still be verified.

<Steps>
  <Step title="Receive a credential">
    The wallet receives a credential from an issuer, which is the organization that creates it. Examples include an airline, employer, bank, or event organizer.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Store it with the holder key">
    The wallet keeps the credential and the private key that proves the wallet controls it. A private key is a secret the wallet uses to sign presentations.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Review the request">
    A verifier asks for specific facts, such as `age_over_18` or `boarding_group`. The wallet should show the holder what will be shared.
  </Step>
  <Step title="Present only the approved facts">
    The wallet sends the selected facts plus proof that this presentation belongs to the current request.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Note>
  In Didit ID, verification requests use OpenID for Verifiable Presentations (OpenID4VP). This is the standard way a verifier asks a wallet to present a credential.
</Note>

## Why this is useful

Most checks do not need every fact on a document. A venue may need to know that you are old enough to enter. It does not need your address, full name, or birth date.

Selective disclosure reduces oversharing. The verifier gets the fact it needs, and the holder keeps unrelated facts private.

<Tip>
  A good wallet screen should show who is asking, which issuer made the credential, and exactly which claims will be shared.
</Tip>
