---
title: "What is a verifiable credential?"
description: "Understand a verifiable credential through ID cards, diplomas, boarding passes, and a wax seal."
---

A verifiable credential is a tamper-proof digital version of a physical ID card, diploma, membership card, ticket, or boarding pass. It contains facts, such as a name, ticket number, age check result, or employee role. The important part is that someone else can check where it came from and whether it was changed.

Think of a school diploma. The school gives it to a student. Later, an employer looks at the diploma and checks that it really came from that school.

With Didit ID, the same idea happens online. The issuer gives the holder a digital credential. Later, a verifier checks the credential before trusting the facts inside it.

## Why it cannot be forged

A physical certificate may use a stamp, raised seal, or special paper. A verifiable credential uses a cryptographic signature, which is a digital seal made with math.

The simple analogy is a wax seal. Only the issuer can make that exact seal. Anyone can check that the seal is real, and the seal breaks if someone changes the message.

<Steps>
  <Step title="The issuer writes the facts">
    The issuer writes claims, which are facts inside the credential. For example, an airline may write `flight_number`, `seat`, and `boarding_group`.
  </Step>
  <Step title="The issuer seals the credential">
    The issuer signs the credential. This is like pressing its unique wax seal into the credential.
  </Step>
  <Step title="The holder keeps it">
    The holder stores the credential in a wallet app. The holder is the person or system the credential is about.
  </Step>
  <Step title="The verifier checks it">
    The verifier checks the seal before trusting the facts. If the facts changed, the check fails.
  </Step>
</Steps>

<Note>
  A signature proves who issued the credential and whether it changed. It does not automatically mean every verifier should trust that issuer. Trust is a separate choice.
</Note>

## Why this matters

Paper proof is slow. People scan documents, upload files, wait for reviews, or call the original source. That process leaks more personal data than needed and often needs to be repeated.

A verifiable credential lets the holder reuse a trusted proof. It also lets the verifier ask for only the facts it needs. For example, a venue can check that someone is over 18 without seeing their full birth date.

<Tip>
  A verifiable credential is not just a picture of a document. It is a signed digital proof that software can check.
</Tip>
